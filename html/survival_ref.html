3 MINUTES — AIR / BLEEDING

Without oxygen, brain damage begins in 3-5 minutes. Uncontrolled arterial bleeding kills in minutes. Secure airway. Apply tourniquet for limb bleeding.

3 HOURS — CORE TEMPERATURE

Hypothermia kills in 3 hours in harsh conditions. Get out of wind/rain/cold. Small shelter retains body heat. Ground insulation critical — ground steals heat 25x faster than air.

3 DAYS — WATER

Dehydration rapidly impairs judgment. Find/purify water within 3 days. Drink before thirsty. 1L/day minimum survival, 3L+ normal activity, 6L in heat/exertion.

3 WEEKS — FOOD

Body survives weeks without food (with water). Food is lowest survival priority. Focus on shelter, water, signaling first. Foraging or trapping for calories.

PRIORITIES IN ORDER

1. Stay calm — panic kills. 2. Stop/stabilize injuries. 3. Signal for rescue (start early). 4. Find/build shelter. 5. Find water. 6. Find food. Most rescues happen within 72 hours — stay put if possible.

FIRE TRIANGLE

Fuel + Heat + Oxygen. Remove any element to extinguish. Build wind-protected structure. Start small, grow the fire. Have tinder, kindling, and fuel ready before lighting.

TINDER MATERIALS

Best: birch bark, dry grass, cattail fluff, pine resin, fatwood shavings, cedar bark shredded fine, dry fungi. Must be bone dry. Prepare more than you think you need.

FIRE STARTING — PRIORITY ORDER

1. Lighter or waterproof matches (always carry both)
2. Ferro rod — works when wet, lasts 12,000+ strikes
3. Magnifying lens (sun only)
4. Friction: bow drill (willow/cedar board, willow spindle)
5. Battery + steel wool

BOW DRILL TECHNIQUE

Board: dry softwood. Spindle: same wood. Cut notch to edge of burn circle. Tinder bundle under notch. Consistent downward pressure + speed. Coal forms in 30-60 seconds of sustained effort. Transfer to bundle gently, blow softly from below.

WATER SOURCE PRIORITY

Running water > spring > still water > ground seepage > dew/rain collection > transpiration bags. Avoid stagnant pools. Higher elevation = cleaner. Moving water = safer.

PURIFICATION METHODS

Boiling: rolling boil 1 min (3 min at altitude). Chemical: 8 drops unscented bleach/gallon or iodine tablets. UV: clear container in direct sunlight 6+ hours. Filter: improvised charcoal-sand-gravel layers (sediment only, still purify).

FINDING WATER

Follow drainages downhill. Look for animal trails converging. Birds fly toward water at dawn/dusk. Vegetation: willows, cottonwoods, cattails = water nearby. Dig in dry stream bed at outside of bend.

DEHYDRATION SIGNS

Dark urine, dry mouth, fatigue (early) → headache, dizziness, rapid pulse (moderate) → confusion, no urination, rapid breathing (severe — emergency). Drink before you're thirsty in hot conditions.

SHELTER PRIORITIES

1. Wind protection. 2. Waterproofing. 3. Ground insulation (CRITICAL). 4. Size: small retains body heat better. Location: avoid drainages (flash floods), widow-makers, hilltops (lightning). Slope for drainage.

LEAN-TO

Simplest. Ridge pole between two trees. Branches/debris at 45° angle. Layer leaves/bark 12"+ thick. Reflective fire in front multiplies heat. Open side faces fire, away from wind.

DEBRIS HUT

Best all-weather shelter. 8-9ft ridgepole, ribbing at 45°, pile debris 3-4ft thick all sides. Crawl-in size only. Fill inside with dry debris bedding. One person generates enough heat to survive near-freezing with no fire.

GROUND INSULATION

Minimum 4-6 inches dry debris under body. More in cold. Pine boughs, dry leaves, dry grass. A sleeping bag or space blanket loses 80% insulation when compressed against cold ground without padding.

UNIVERSAL DISTRESS: GROUPS OF 3

3 fires, 3 whistle blasts, 3 gunshots, 3 flashes. Repeat after pause. Internationally recognized. Ground-to-air: X = need medical. V = need help. Arrow = traveling direction. Use rocks, logs, trampled vegetation visible from air.

SIGNAL MIRROR

Most effective daytime signal — visible 10+ miles. Aim: hold mirror near eye, see reflection dot on target. Angle to catch sun. Flash slowly. Works on cloudy days with diffuse light.

FIRE SIGNALS

3 fires in triangle 60ft apart = distress. Day: add green vegetation for white smoke. Night: maintain bright flame. Clear debris in 20-30ft diameter opening for visibility. Mark with reflective material.

STAY PUT vs MOVE

Stay put if: someone knows your plan, you have shelter/water, injured/weak. Move if: certain rescue isn't coming, know route to safety, no water at current location. Leave trail markers if moving.

NATURAL COMPASS

Sun: rises E, sets W, due south at noon (N hemisphere). Shadows point N at noon. North Star (Polaris): find Big Dipper, follow the pointer stars 5x their distance = Polaris = true north. Moss: heaviest growth on north side.

STICK SHADOW METHOD

Place vertical stick. Mark shadow tip (A). Wait 15 minutes, mark new tip (B). Line A to B = east-west (A=west, B=east). Perpendicular = north-south. Works anywhere with sun.

TERRAIN READING

Water flows to valleys/rivers, rivers to civilization. Ridgelines lead to passes. Powerlines and cut lines lead to roads. Follow streams — they merge and grow. Avoid crossing ridgelines unnecessarily — exhausting.

MAINTAIN DIRECTION

Pick distant landmark, walk to it, pick next. Look back frequently to check your back trail stays straight. Leave trail markers: cairns, broken branches, scratched arrows on trees. Without landmarks: count steps, maintain compass bearing.

SQUARE KNOT (Joining Two Ropes)

Right over left, then left over right. Flat and symmetric. Good for bandages. Slips under load — not for critical loads. "Square" shape = correct. "Granny" shape = wrong (asymmetric).

BOWLINE (Fixed Loop)

"Rabbit comes up out of hole, around the tree, back in hole." Makes non-slipping loop. Used for rescue, tying to anchor. Pull knot tight after forming. Cannot be untied under load.

CLOVE HITCH (Fastening to Post)

Two half hitches around a pole. Quick to tie/untie. Use as start for lashing. Add extra half hitch for security. Good for temporary anchors. Slips under constant lateral load.

PRUSIK (Ascending a Rope)

Loop around main rope 3x, pass through itself. Grips under load, slides when unweighted. Useful for ascending rope, foot loops. Must be smaller diameter than main rope.
